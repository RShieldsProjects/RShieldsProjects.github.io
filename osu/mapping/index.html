With all that garbage out of the way, let's start by asking a simple question.

Why are all maps the same?

I'm not kidding. All maps made in 2014 and later use the same concepts. Like, rhythms are similar, patterns are similar, slider shapes are similar, etc.

I call this style "modern mapping style." Compared to "old mapping style," modern maps use freeform placement and predictable rhythms. Patterns are about aesthetics instead of perfectly representing the music, and sliderends often land on nothing.

Perhaps the first modern map was The Pretender by RLC. This map won map of the year and singlehandedly changed many players' opinions of what maps should look and play like.

Why learn modern style?

Even if you hate the modern mapping meta, you still need to learn it because you need to know what expectations current players will have when they open your map for the first time.

If you want to map in old style, your maps will have to be different from true old maps since many patterns from that time have become unreadable, and many patterns that used to be awful are now easy.

Creative Mapping

Is creative mapping dead? No way. If you look at mappers respected by other mappers, you'll find plenty of cool stuff being made all the time.

Regardless of if you want your maps to be creative or pp farms, you will need to know the basics. All the concepts covered in this guide are important for any kind of mapping. And if you want to break the rules, you have to know the rules first.

To that effect, I've set up this guide around making a "TV Size" map (think, anime opening). Not all TV Size songs are mapped like this and not all maps like this are on TV Size songs. However, this style is so closely associated with TV Size songs that you can ask mappers about "TV Size mapping" and they'll know what you're talking about.

(Honestly, I find TV Size maps boring. But they're easier to study because they're straightforward.)

Setting Up osu! for Mapping

The settings you use when playing the game may be bad for mapping. Here are the Editor Options that you should use:

  • No Snaking Sliders
  • No Hit Animations
  • Yes Follow Points
  • Yes Stacking

In addition, the skin you map with must have:

  • No custom hitsounds
  • Yes combo numbers and colors

You can map with the skin you play with if it follows those rules. Some people even use the default skin.

I made the skin that I use: Tabula 0.2.1

Getting into the Right Mindset

Mapping is a tough process. Your first map will be awful and so will your second and third. But, if you stick with it, you will get better.

Consider pishifat's experiences:

Improvement is slow. Even if you know about every problem in your first map, you won't fix all of those problems on your second. Focus on one thing at a time. When you're really good at rhythm, you won't have to think about it. After that, you can focus on spacing, etc.

You'll get more experience if you remap instead of fixing your old maps. Often, you'd have to change so much to get a better pattern that you might as well start fresh.

You'll be making a lot of maps in the beginning, most of which you shouldn't upload since you can only have 4 pending maps at once. Instead, export your map as a .osz, find a good file sharing service, and send via link. Sometimes, you won't even need feedback. In those cases, you don' tneed to look for any.
