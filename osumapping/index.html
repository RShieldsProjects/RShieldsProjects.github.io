With all that garbage out of the way, let's start by asking a simple question.

Why are all maps the same?

I'm not kidding. When you look at maps made in 2014 and later, they all tend to have similar concepts. Rhythms are similar among similar genres, patterns pretty similar, slider shapes are similar.

I can pretty comfortably say that all maps ranked after 2014 are in what I like to call "modern mapping style." Compared to "old mapping style," modern maps feature relatively freeform placement and relatively predictable rhythms. Slider shapes lean more toward aesthetics than prefectly representing the music, and sliderends have stopped landing on strong sounds.

Perhaps the first modern map, and certainly one of the better-known maps among the mapping community is The Pretender by RLC. RLC is particularly known for the previously noted style choices that now define modern mapping, and it's fair to say he singlehandedly changed many players' opinions of what maps should look and play like.

Why learn modern style?

Even if you hate the modern mapping meta, you still need to learn it because you need to know what kinds of expectations current players will have when they open your map for the first time.

If you go on to map in the old style, your maps will have to be different from true old maps since many patterns from that time have become unreadable now and many patterns that players now find easy were previously unacceptable.

Creative Mapping

Is creative mapping dead? Not by a long shot. If you don't follow the mapping scene, you might see lots of pp mapping and think nobody's working on the crazy stuff. Behind the scenes, however, creative mapping has never been better.

Regardless of if you want your maps to end up on the creative side or as pp farms, you'll still need to know the basics. Almost all of the concepts covered in this guide are important for any kind of mapping. If you want to break the rules, you have to know the rules.

To that effect, I've set up this guide around mapping a "TV Size" song. While not all TV Size songs are mapped are in the style I present and not all songs mapped in this style are TV Size, the style is so closely associated with TV Size songs that you can ask mappers about "TV Size mapping" and they'll know what you're talking about.

Once again, you must learn the basics by mapping super boring stuff before you can get to interesting mapping.

Setting Up osu! for Mapping

The settings you might use for playing the game aren't necessarily the best for mapping. Luckily, osu! has separate editor settings from gameplay settings. Here are the Editor Options that you should definitely use:

  • No Snaking Sliders
  • No Hit Animations
  • Yes Follow Points
  • Yes Stacking

In addition, the skin you play with may not be optimal to map with. There are two extremely important characteristics of a skin that you can use for mapping:

  • It cannot have custom hitsounds because not everybody uses the same hitsounds.
  • It must have combo numbers and use the map's combo colors since New Combos are very important in mapping.

Otherwise, the most important part of a mapping skin is that you're comfortable with it. Some people even use the default skin (there's an toggle for it in the Editor Options), but on Insane and above, you can count on players not to use the default skin when playing.

Getting into the Right Mindset

Mapping is a brutal process. Your first map will inevitably be awful and so will your second and third. But, if you stick to it, you will get better.

That being said, you'll get more experience if you prefer to remap over fixing previous maps. Non-scientific experiments have shown that people tend to improve faster when they emphasize quantity over quality. This doesn't mean that you shouldn't learn from your mistakes, just that you shouldn't spend too much time polishing something that you could create a better replacement for.

Mapping is also an iterative process. Just because you can identify all the problems with your first map doesn't mean your second map should strive to fix all of those problems. Get good at one thing and apply that on every map afterward, then get good at something else.

You'll be making a lot of maps in the beginning, most of which you shouldn't upload since you can only have 4 pending maps at once. Instead, export your map as a .osz, find a good file sharing service, and send via link. Even then, unless you're really confused, more practice may often be better than feedback.
